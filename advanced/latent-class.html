Latent Class / Profile Analysis

Advanced Topics

Latent Class Analysis (LCA) and Latent Profile Analysis (LPA) are person-centred approaches that identify unobserved subgroups in a population — “classes” or “profiles” of individuals who respond similarly across a set of indicators and differently from people in other subgroups. In contrast to variable-centred methods (factor analysis, regression), which describe how variables relate, LCA and LPA describe how people cluster (Collins & Lanza, 2010, §1.2.3).

Technique Indicator type Typical application
LCA Categorical (binary or polytomous) Classifying respondents by symptom-presence patterns, diagnostic profiles, response-pattern groups
LPA Continuous Identifying motivational profiles or trait profiles from several continuous scale scores

Statistically, both are forms of finite mixture modelling: the assumption is that the population is a mixture of k unobserved subgroups, each with its own response distribution, and the data don’t tell you which subgroup each respondent belongs to.

The LCA model

Following Collins & Lanza (2010, §2.5), the LCA model has two kinds of parameters:

  • Latent class prevalences (γ): the proportion of the population belonging to each class. With C classes, C − 1 are estimated (the last one is determined by 1 minus the sum).
  • Item-response probabilities (ρ): for each item and each response category, the probability of giving that response conditional on being in a particular class. With J items each having Rj categories and C classes, C · Σ(Rj − 1) item-response probabilities are estimated.

The LPA analogue replaces the item-response probabilities with within-class means and variances for each continuous indicator.

Local independence — the defining assumption

LCA assumes that conditional on the latent class, the observed indicators are independent of each other (Collins & Lanza, 2010, §2.5.2). Any correlation between two items is, under the model, entirely explained by their shared dependence on the latent class. If two items remain correlated even within a class, the local-independence assumption is violated and you’ll likely need to either (a) extract more classes, (b) drop an item, or (c) freely estimate a residual covariance.

TipWhy local independence is the LCA analogue of common-factor models

A CFA models indicator covariances as arising from latent factors plus residuals. LCA models indicator covariances as arising entirely from class membership. Local independence is the corresponding assumption — if violated, the model is mis-specified the same way a CFA is mis-specified by an unmodelled cross-loading.

Estimation

LCA and LPA parameters are estimated via Maximum Likelihood, almost always using the EM algorithm (Collins & Lanza, 2010, Ch 4). Two specifically LCA-ish estimation gotchas:

Label switching

The model is invariant to relabelling of the classes — calling one class “high-risk” and another “low-risk” vs the reverse gives statistically identical likelihoods. EM can converge to either labelling, and across multiple runs the labelling can change between solutions. This is harmless for the likelihood but it makes parameter comparisons across runs / draws messy. Report the substantive labels you’ve assigned to classes (based on their item-response probabilities), not the software-default numbering.

Multiple starting values are mandatory

The LCA likelihood is multi-modal — local maxima are common. Always run from many (e.g. 100–1000) random starting values and keep the solution with the highest likelihood (Collins & Lanza, 2010, §4.4–4.5). If your “best” solution is not replicated across multiple starts, the model is empirically unidentified at that class size — try fewer classes or simplify the model.

How many classes?

This is the central question in LCA, and there is no single oracle (Collins & Lanza, 2010, §4.6). The standard workflow is to fit a series of solutions (1 class, 2 classes, …, K classes) and compare them on:

Information criteria

  • BIC (Bayesian information criterion) — usually the default recommended index for LCA; penalises model complexity more heavily than AIC and tends to favour parsimonious solutions.
  • aBIC (sample-size adjusted BIC; Sclove, 1987) — modified for smaller samples.
  • AIC — tends to over-extract classes (favours more complex models) and is less commonly used for class enumeration.
  • CAIC — between BIC and AIC in penalty.

Lower is better for all of these. Plot them as a line across k and look for an elbow (a sharp slowing of improvement) — the k at the elbow is the parsimonious choice.

Likelihood-ratio tests for k vs (k − 1) classes

The standard χ² difference test does not apply to comparing mixture models with different numbers of classes (regularity conditions are violated). Use instead:

  • Lo-Mendell-Rubin adjusted LRT (LMR) — a parametric correction. Significant p means k classes fit better than k − 1.
  • Bootstrap Likelihood Ratio Test (BLRT) — non-parametric; widely considered more reliable. Same interpretation.

Both are available in Mplus.

G² and absolute fit (for LCA only)

The likelihood-ratio G² statistic compares observed and expected response-pattern frequencies against a χ² distribution (Collins & Lanza, 2010, §4.3.2). Use with caution: with even modestly many items, the response-pattern contingency table becomes sparse (N / W < 5), and the χ² approximation breaks down. Sparseness is the rule, not the exception — most LCA solutions can’t be tested for absolute fit this way.

How well does each class hang together?

Even after picking k, you should check that the classes are substantively interpretable and well-separated.

Homogeneity

A class is homogeneous if its item-response probabilities are close to 0 or close to 1 — i.e. members of the class share a clear response profile (Collins & Lanza, 2010, §3.3). Probabilities clustered around .5 mean the class doesn’t have a distinct profile on that item.

Latent class separation

Separation is the extent to which the classes have distinguishable item-response patterns. High homogeneity within classes plus large differences in those probabilities between classes = good separation. You can have high homogeneity but poor separation (two classes are both homogeneous but look similar) — in which case the second class isn’t really informative.

Posterior classification quality

Each respondent has an estimated posterior probability of belonging to each class. A model with good separation will assign respondents to classes with posteriors near 1; a model with poor separation will produce posteriors near 1/k (essentially guessing).

Diagnostic Definition Threshold for “good”
Mean posterior probability per class Average posterior of being in class c, among respondents assigned to c ≥ .80 (rule of thumb)
Odds of correct classification (OCC) The class-specific assignment accuracy normalised by chance (Nagin, 2005) ≥ 5 per class (Nagin, 2005, pp. 88–89)
Entropy (Ramaswamy et al., 1993) A single-number summary of overall classification certainty; 0 ≤ E ≤ 1 Higher = better separation
WarningDon’t use entropy for class enumeration

Entropy is useful for describing the separation quality of a chosen model — but it tends to decrease as the number of classes increases for purely artefactual reasons. Picking the k with highest entropy will systematically under-extract classes. Use BIC and LRT-based tests for class enumeration, and entropy for post-hoc interpretation only (Collins & Lanza, 2010, §3.5).

Beyond a single cross-section

Multiple-group LCA

Asks whether the same latent class structure holds across groups (e.g. men vs women, cohorts), and whether classes are equally prevalent across them. The analogue of measurement invariance testing in CFA: fit a configural model and progressively constrain item-response probabilities to equality (Collins & Lanza, 2010, Ch 5). Partial measurement invariance is also possible (§5.10).

LCA with covariates

Once classes are identified, you may want to predict class membership from individual-level covariates (sex, age, intervention condition) — this is a multinomial logistic regression of class membership on the covariates. Two ways to do this: (a) the one-step approach, where covariates enter the LCA model directly, and (b) the three-step approach (Vermunt, 2010), where you fit the LCA, assign cases to their most likely class, and regress class on covariates with a correction for assignment error. The three-step approach is now the standard recommendation (Collins & Lanza, 2010, Ch 6).

Latent Transition Analysis (LTA)

The longitudinal extension. Each respondent is in some class at each measurement occasion, and the model estimates transition probabilities between classes across occasions (Collins & Lanza, 2010, Ch 7–8). Useful for studying stage-sequential development, recovery patterns, intervention effects on group membership.

How to do it in JASP

JASP does not currently have a dedicated LCA / LPA module in its standard distribution. For these analyses, the field’s standard software is:

  • Mplus (Muthén & Muthén) — the de facto reference for mixture modelling. Long history; comprehensive feature set (multiple-group, covariates, three-step, LTA, growth mixture models). Commercial.
  • R poLCA (Linzer & Lewis, 2011) — open-source LCA for categorical indicators.
  • R tidyLPA (Rosenberg et al.) — accessible LPA wrapper around mclust; integrates with the tidyverse.
  • R mclust (Scrucca et al., 2016) — general-purpose Gaussian mixture modelling, including LPA.
  • R depmixS4 — LCA + hidden Markov models for transitions over time.
  • Latent GOLD — another commercial alternative with a strong GUI.

If you’re new to mixture modelling and have continuous indicators, tidyLPA is the gentlest entry point. For categorical LCA in R, poLCA is the standard.

Reporting

TipReporting an LCA / LPA study

At minimum:

  1. Conceptual rationale: why a person-centred / mixture approach for this question, not a variable-centred one.
  2. Indicators: the set of items / scales used as class indicators, with descriptive statistics and (for LCA) frequency tables.
  3. Model selection: a table of fit indices (BIC, aBIC, AIC, LMR, BLRT, entropy) for k = 1, 2, …, K classes; explicit justification for the chosen k combining statistical fit and interpretability.
  4. Estimation details: software + version, number of random starting values used, whether the best log-likelihood was replicated.
  5. Class characterisation: the item-response probabilities (LCA) or within-class means and SDs (LPA), in a table or profile plot; a substantive label per class.
  6. Class quality: mean posterior probability per class, OCC values, and overall entropy.
  7. Substantive interpretation: what each class represents, with appropriate hedging if separation is modest.
  8. For multi-group / covariate / longitudinal extensions: details of which approach was used (one-step vs three-step; configural vs invariant; etc.).

Wiley. — The standard reference: model + notation (Ch 2), measurement quality (Ch 3), estimation and model selection (Ch 4), multi-group LCA (Ch 5), covariates (Ch 6), latent transition models (Ch 7–8).
  • Masyn, K. E. (2013). Latent class analysis and finite mixture modeling. In T. D. Little (Ed.), The Oxford handbook of quantitative methods (Vol. 2). Oxford University Press. — Compact chapter-length treatment with up-to-date model-selection recommendations.
  • Hagenaars, J. A., & McCutcheon, A. L. (Eds.). (2002). Applied latent class analysis. Cambridge University Press. — Edited handbook with substantive applications.
  • Nylund-Gibson, K., & Choi, A. Y. (2018). Ten frequently asked questions about latent class analysis. Translational Issues in Psychological Science, 4(4), 440–461. — Excellent FAQ-style introduction.
  • Berlin, K. S., Williams, N. A., & Parra, G. R. (2014). An introduction to latent variable mixture modeling (part 1): Overview and cross-sectional latent class and latent profile analyses. Journal of Pediatric Psychology, 39(2), 174–187. — Accessible applied tutorial.
  • Vermunt, J. K. (2010). Latent class modeling with covariates: Two improved three-step approaches. Political Analysis, 18(4), 450–469. — The three-step covariate approach.
  • Nagin, D. S. (2005). Group-based modeling of development. Harvard University Press. — Source of the OCC diagnostic.
