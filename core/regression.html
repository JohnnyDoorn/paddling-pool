Correlation & Regression

Modified

4 June 2026

Basics

Regression allows you to model the relationship between a continuous outcome variable (the dependent variable, DV) and one or more predictors (the independent variables, IVs). Start with correlation to quantify the relationship between two variables, then move to regression when you want to predict an outcome or test the unique contribution of each predictor.

This page covers:

  • Correlation — quantifying a relationship between two continuous variables
  • Simple regression — predicting an outcome from one predictor
  • Multiple regression — predicting an outcome from several predictors, controlling for the others
TipRegression is ANOVA, just continuous

Quick framing: regression keeps the same explained-variance logic from ANOVA, but swaps categorical for continuous predictors — and JASP’s Regression module instead of ANOVA.

Correlation

TipResearch vignette — which student traits predict the kind of lecturer they like?

Chamorro-Premuzic et al. (2008) measured five student personality traits (Big Five) and five dimensions of preferred lecturer style (knowledgeable, charismatic, accessible, etc.). The natural first move is a full correlation matrix — every trait against every lecturer dimension — to see which pairings hang together and in what direction. Source: Labcoat Leni (DSUJ ch. 7).

→ Worked through in Exercise 2 below.

What is it?

A correlation coefficient (r) describes the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two continuous variables. Pearson’s r ranges from −1 (perfect negative) to +1 (perfect positive), with 0 indicating no linear relationship.

r Conventional label
±.10 Small
±.30 Medium
±.50 Large

When to use it

  • As a descriptive measure of association between two variables (no implied causality)
  • As a preliminary step before regression
  • When reporting relationships in your results section

Assumptions

  • Both variables are continuous (interval/ratio)
  • The relationship is linear (check with a scatter plot — non-linear relationships yield misleading r values)
  • Approximately bivariate normal — no extreme outliers (outliers can inflate or deflate r dramatically)

If assumptions are violated, use Spearman’s ρ (rho) — a rank-based non-parametric alternative available in JASP.

Simple regression

TipResearch vignette — does advertising spend predict album sales?

A music label tracked the advertising budget (€) and first-week album sales for a sample of releases. A simple linear regression of sales on advertising estimates two numbers: the predicted sales of an album that gets no advertising (the intercept b₀), and how much each extra Euro of advertising buys you in expected sales (the slope b₁). The same dataset returns later on this page in the APA reporting example. Source: DSUJ ch. 8 (album sales running example).

What is it?

Simple (linear) regression models the relationship between one predictor (X) and a continuous outcome (Y):

\[Y = b_0 + b_1 X + \varepsilon\]

  • b₀: the intercept — the predicted value of Y when X = 0
  • b₁: the slope — the expected change in Y for a one-unit increase in X
  • ε: the residual error

The R² statistic tells you the proportion of variance in Y explained by the model. Adjusted R² penalises for the number of predictors and is used for model comparison.

TipWhy the intercept (b₀) belongs in the model

You might be tempted to drop the intercept when it isn’t substantively meaningful (e.g. when X = 0 makes no sense). Don’t — omitting it forces the regression line through the origin, which introduces systematic over- and under-estimation. The reported R² can then look misleadingly high because it’s compared against a degenerate null model that predicts zero rather than the mean.

→ Worked example with album sales

Assumptions

  • Linearity: the relationship between X and Y is linear — check with a scatter plot
  • Independence of residuals: each observation is independent — check with the Durbin-Watson statistic
  • Homoscedasticity: the spread of residuals is constant across fitted values — check the residuals vs. fitted plot
  • Normality of residuals: residuals should be approximately normally distributed — check the residuals Q-Q plot
  • No influential outliers: cases with high Cook’s distance (> 1 as a rough rule) warrant investigation
TipDiagnosing homoscedasticity at a glance

Plot the standardised residuals against the standardised predictions. A “healthy” cloud of points means error is constant across the range of Y. A funnel shape or a tilted line means the model is more accurate for some predicted values than others — a sign of mis-specification.

→ Visual catalogue: healthy vs. heteroscedastic plots

Multiple regression

TipResearch vignette — does narcissism predict Facebook status frequency over and above extraversion?

Ong et al. (2011) measured adolescents’ status-update frequency, demographics (age, grade, sex), extraversion, and narcissism. The interesting question isn’t whether narcissism correlates with status posts — extraversion does too, and the two traits overlap. A hierarchical multiple regression enters demographics first (Model 0), extraversion next (Model 1), and narcissism last (Model 2); the ΔR² at each step shows what each block adds over and above what’s already in. Source: Labcoat Leni (DSUJ ch. 8).

→ Worked through in Exercise 3 below.

What is it?

Multiple regression extends simple regression to two or more predictors:

\[Y = b_0 + b_1 X_1 + b_2 X_2 + \ldots + b_k X_k + \varepsilon\]

Each bⱼ is a partial regression coefficient — it represents the unique effect of predictor Xⱼ on Y, controlling for all other predictors in the model. This is a crucial distinction from simple regression or from looking at correlations in isolation.

Building your model

There are several approaches to entering predictors:

Method When to use
Simultaneous (Enter) All predictors entered at once. Use when you have theory-driven reasons to include all predictors
Hierarchical Predictors entered in blocks based on theoretical priority. Change in R² (ΔR²) for each block tests the incremental contribution
Stepwise Automated selection based on statistical criteria — generally discouraged for confirmatory research but can be useful exploratory
TipHierarchical regression in JASP

How to set up Model 0 → Model 1 → Model 2 in JASP’s Regression module, what the ΔR² between consecutive models actually means, and why “controlling for X” and “adding X to the null model” are the same operation.

Additional assumption: multicollinearity

When predictors are highly correlated with each other, regression coefficients become unstable. Check: - Tolerance (should be > .1) or VIF (variance inflation factor, should be < 10) — both available in JASP under Collinearity Diagnostics

TipVIF, tolerance, and why 10 isn’t a hard cutoff

What multicollinearity really means (predictors that overlap), how to read VIF (the 10 threshold is a continuum, not a binary pass/fail), and why a scatter plot matrix of your predictors is still worth its weight — VIF only catches linear dependence.

Interpreting the regression output

The key tables in JASP output:

Model Summary

Column What it means
R Multiple correlation coefficient
R² Proportion of variance in Y explained by all predictors combined
Adjusted R² R² corrected for the number of predictors — use this for model comparison
RMSE Root mean square error — average prediction error in the original units

Coefficients table

Column What it means
b (Unstandardised) The slope in original units — “one unit increase in X is associated with a b change in Y, holding all other predictors constant”
β (Standardised) The slope in standard deviation units — comparable across predictors with different scales
t / p Whether the slope is significantly different from zero
95% CI Confidence interval for the slope
TipAPA reporting example

Album sales were significantly predicted by advertising budget, b = 0.096, 95% CI [0.076, 0.115], β = .578, t(196) = 9.98, p < .001. The model explained 33% of the variance in album sales, F(1, 198) = 99.6, p < .001, adjusted R² = .33.

Dummy coding categorical predictors

Continuous predictors enter regression directly. Categorical predictors must be dummy coded: create k − 1 binary variables (0/1), where k is the number of categories. JASP does this automatically when you define a variable as nominal.

Example: a variable with 3 groups (control, therapy A, therapy B) → 2 dummy variables. The reference category (control) becomes the baseline against which the others are compared.

TipHow predictions and group means come out of the dummies

The intercept becomes the mean of the reference group; each dummy’s coefficient is the difference from that group. Add a covariate (or another categorical predictor) and the coefficients shift slightly — the dummies no longer recover the group means exactly, but the prediction formula still works the same way. With an interaction term added, you do recover each cell mean — at the cost of more parameters.
