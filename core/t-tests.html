T-Tests

Modified

11 June 2026

Basics

t-tests are used to compare means — either against a known value, or between two groups. They are one of the most widely used inferential tests in psychology.

Three types of t-test

Test Research question Example
One-sample Does my sample mean differ from a known value? Is the mean IQ of my sample different from the population mean of 100?
Paired-samples Do two related measurements differ? Do anxiety scores decrease from pre- to post-therapy within the same participants?
Independent-samples Do two independent groups differ? Do males and females differ in their extraversion scores?

Research vignettes — what each test can answer

TipOne-sample t-test — are people any good at guessing their own IQ?

Students report what they think their IQ is. The IQ scale is constructed to have a population mean of 100, so a one-sample t-test against 100 asks whether students systematically over- or under-estimate themselves. A one-sentence test of a popular folk-psychology claim. From SSR Lecture 11; the same setup appears in DSUJ ch. 9.

TipPaired-samples t-test — does festival hygiene drop over the weekend?

The same festival-goers were rated for hygiene on day 1 and day 3 of a UK rock festival. Each participant gives two scores, so a paired-samples t-test on the day-1 − day-3 differences asks whether the typical attendee gets visibly grottier. DSUJ ch. 9 uses this dataset to introduce paired tests, then immediately points out that the differences are skewed enough to want a Wilcoxon as a sanity check.

→ Worked in Exercise 3 below.

TipIndependent-samples t-test — are senior business managers as histrionic as clinical inpatients?

Board & Fritzon (2005) compared personality profiles of senior business managers with a clinical sample of inpatients diagnosed with personality disorders. Each participant belongs to one group, so an independent-samples t-test on the histrionic scale asks whether boardroom and ward differ on a trait often associated with executive charisma. From Labcoat Leni (DSUJ ch. 9).

When to use which t-test

  • One-sample: you have a single group and want to compare its mean to a fixed reference value (e.g., population norm, chance level, zero)
  • Paired-samples (also called dependent-samples or repeated-measures t-test): the same participants measured twice, or participants matched in pairs — the two measurements are not independent
  • Independent-samples: two separate, unrelated groups — each participant contributes one score
WarningDon’t confuse paired and independent

Using an independent-samples t-test when you have a repeated-measures design throws away half the information and inflates your error term. Always identify whether measurements within rows of your dataset are linked.

TipThe paired t-test is just a one-sample t-test on difference scores

For each participant, compute the difference between their two measurements. Now you have one set of difference scores — and you’re back to a one-sample t-test against H₀: mean difference = 0. This is why “paired”, “dependent-samples”, and “within-subjects” all refer to the same procedure.

One-sided or two-sided?

Whichever t-test you pick, you’ll also need to decide what alternative hypothesis to test:

  • Two-sided (default in JASP): H₁ says the mean differs from the null value in either direction. Use this unless you have a strong directional prediction.
  • One-sided (directional): H₁ says the mean is either greater than, or less than, the null value — you commit to a direction. More power if you’re right; if the effect goes the other way, you can’t reject H₀.
TipA one-sided test is a commitment, not a shortcut

Commit to a one-sided test before looking at your data — ideally pre-registered. Switching to one-sided after seeing a result in your favoured direction is a common form of p-hacking.

→ Visual explainer: how the rejection region changes between one-sided and two-sided

TipChoosing your alternative hypothesis

Using the IQ self-assessment example: two-sided (“self-reported IQ is different from 120”) asks whether there’s any bias, while one-sided (“students systematically overestimate IQ”) tests a specific direction. Different research questions, different rejection regions.

Assumptions

  • Scale data: the dependent variable is continuous (interval or ratio level)
  • Independence of observations: each row in the dataset is an independent participant (violated in paired designs, which is why you use the paired t-test instead)
  • Normality: the dependent variable (or, for paired data, the difference scores) should be approximately normally distributed, especially important in small samples (n < 30)
  • Equal variances (independent-samples only): Levene’s test checks this. JASP reports a Welch correction by default, which is robust to unequal variances — this is generally the safer choice
TipWhy “always use Welch” is the safer default

A subtle point: significance tests for assumptions (Levene’s, Shapiro-Wilk) are themselves driven by sample size. At large n, even a trivial deviation from equal variances will make Levene’s significant — that doesn’t mean you have a “problem”. At small n, neither test has the power to detect a meaningful violation. The recommended workflow: look at the observed standard deviations (a ratio of more than ~1.4 between groups is worth noting), inspect Q-Q plots, and just default to the Welch t-test — it costs you a little power and buys robustness against unequal variances.

Checking normality

Use the Q-Q plot and inspect skewness/kurtosis in Descriptives (see the Descriptives page). The Shapiro-Wilk test is available in JASP under the assumption checks, but is sensitive to sample size — a visual check is often more informative.

Effect size

Always report an effect size alongside your p-value. For t-tests, use Cohen’s d:

d Conventional label
0.2 Small
0.5 Medium
0.8 Large

These benchmarks are rough guides — the meaningful effect size depends on your research context. Tick Cohen’s d under Effect Size in the JASP options panel.

Interpreting the output

The JASP output for an independent-samples t-test contains:

  • t: the test statistic — the ratio of the mean difference to the standard error of the difference
  • df: degrees of freedom (for Welch’s test, this will not be a whole number)
  • p: the two-tailed p-value. Reject H₀ (no difference) if p < your α level (typically .05)
  • Mean difference: the raw difference between group means — report this alongside the SE or CI
  • Cohen’s d: the effect size
TipAPA reporting example

An independent-samples t-test showed that the anxiety ratings of participants shown a picture of a spider (M = 47.0, SD = 9.3) were significantly higher than those of participants shown a picture of a real spider (M = 40.0, SD = 9.3), t(21.4) = 1.68, p = .108, d = 0.71. (This is an example — adjust numbers and interpretation to your own results.)

Non-parametric alternative

If your data strongly violate the normality assumption (and your sample is small), use the Wilcoxon tests instead:

t-test Non-parametric equivalent
One-sample Wilcoxon signed-rank test
Paired-samples Wilcoxon signed-rank test
Independent-samples Mann–Whitney U test (Wilcoxon rank-sum test)

These are available under T-Tests → Nonparametric in JASP.
