Descriptive Statistics & Visualisation

Modified

19 June 2026

Basics

Before running any inferential test, look at your data. Descriptives and plots tell you the shape of the distribution, flag outliers, and show whether the assumptions of your next analysis hold. Surprising patterns often only show up at this stage.

TipResearch vignette — do fish or cat owners enjoy life more?

A small dataset has two pet groups (fish vs cat), each rated on animal_liking and life_satisfaction. The descriptives table gives you means, SDs, and 95% CIs; a raincloud plot then shows what those numbers hide — maybe one fish owner is dragging the mean up by themselves, or “the cat group” is two clumps with a gap between them. From DSUJ ch. 5 (Smart Alex 5.8).

What are descriptive statistics?

Descriptive statistics summarise the key features of a dataset without making inferences about a population. The most important are:

Central tendency — where is the middle of the distribution?

Statistic Use when
Mean Continuous data, approximately symmetric distribution
Median Skewed distributions or ordinal data
Mode Categorical data, or to describe the most common value

Spread — how much do values vary?

Statistic What it tells you
Standard deviation (SD) Average distance from the mean (same units as the data)
Variance SD² — less intuitive but used in many formulas
Range Max − Min — sensitive to outliers
Interquartile range (IQR) Middle 50% of values — robust to outliers

Shape — is the distribution symmetric?

Statistic What it tells you
Skewness Whether the tail is longer on the left (negative) or right (positive)
Kurtosis How heavy or light the tails are relative to a normal distribution

When to use descriptive statistics

Always, as the first step in every analysis:

  • Before any test: report means and SDs for your key variables
  • To check normality: histograms and Q-Q plots; inspect skewness and kurtosis
  • To detect outliers: boxplots flag values past 1.5 × IQR from the quartiles
  • To describe your sample: demographics (age, gender, group sizes) always need descriptives

Plots — visualising your data

The descriptives table summarises the data; a plot shows it. Which plot to use depends on the measurement level of the variable and whether you’re looking at one distribution or comparing several.

One continuous variable

Plot What it shows When to prefer it
Histogram Shape: skew, modality, gaps, ceiling or floor Always your first look — the eye catches things skewness numbers miss
Boxplot Median, IQR, candidate outliers past the whiskers When the centre and outliers are what you care about
Q-Q plot Sample quantiles vs the theoretical normal quantiles For the normality assumption — straighter line, closer to normal
Density plot A smoothed histogram When n is small and the histogram looks blocky
TipHistogram + boxplot is the workhorse pair

The histogram shows the shape; the boxplot pins down where the action is (median, quartiles, tails). The festival-hygiene example in DSUJ ch. 5 is a nice illustration: the day-1 boxplot flags one extreme outlier off the top of the scale, and the histogram then explains why the mean and median don’t agree.

Comparing continuous distributions across groups

Plot What it shows When to prefer it
Raincloud plot Half-violin density + boxplot + raw points, one strip per group The first plot to reach for. Defaults to this.
Boxplots side-by-side Medians and IQRs together When you have many groups and the raincloud gets crowded
Bar plot with error bars Means + SE or CI per group APA still expects these in papers, but pair with a raincloud while exploring
Scatter with group colour Two continuous variables, group as colour or shape When the group comparison sits inside a continuous relationship
WarningBar-and-error-bar plots hide a lot

Two groups with identical means and SEs can have very different underlying shapes (one bimodal, one symmetric, one carried by a single extreme value). On a bar plot they look the same. SSR Lecture 4 and DSUJ ch. 5 both call this out; rainclouds caught on in psychology as the practical fix.

JASP also has a dedicated Raincloud Plots analysis in the Descriptives module. Drop the continuous outcomes (animal, life_satisfaction) into Dependent Variables and the grouping variable (pet) into Primary Factor to get one density-plus-boxplot-plus-points strip per group:

Two continuous variables

  • Scatter plot — the honest summary of a relationship. Look at it before computing a correlation, not after; non-linearity and extreme points are usually obvious.
  • Scatter-plot matrix — with several continuous variables, JASP’s Plot Matrix in Descriptives gives you every pairwise scatter at once. Useful as a first sweep.

Categorical data

Plot What it shows When to prefer it
Bar plot of counts Frequency of each category The default for a single nominal or ordinal variable
Pie chart Proportions of a whole Rarely the best choice — humans read angles poorly; a bar plot is usually clearer
Stacked / grouped bars One categorical variable split by another The “is X related to Y” picture before a chi-square test
Mosaic plot Cell area proportional to frequency Same data as a stacked bar, but the joint distribution is more visible

In JASP these all live under Descriptives → Plots, or Frequencies → Distribution Plots for the categorical side.

TipPlot first, test after

Anscombe’s quartet — four datasets with identical means, SDs, correlations, and regression lines, but visibly different shapes — is the classic warning that summary statistics can mislead. SSR Lecture 4 walks through it; DSUJ ch. 4 covers the same idea with real psychology data. A p-value never replaces a look at the data.

Assumptions to check

Descriptive statistics have no assumptions themselves, but examining them tells you whether the assumptions of your next analysis hold:

  • Normality: histograms should be roughly bell-shaped; skewness and kurtosis should be close to 0 (rough rule: |value| < 1 for reasonable samples, though this depends on sample size)
  • Outliers: inspect boxplots — individual points beyond the whiskers warrant investigation
  • Floor/ceiling effects: if most values cluster at the minimum or maximum, your scale may not have sufficient range

How to do it in JASP

Open your dataset, go to Descriptives → Descriptives in the analysis menu.

Interpreting the output

After running Descriptives in JASP, the output table shows your requested statistics. Key things to check:

  • N: the number of valid (non-missing) observations per variable
  • Mean ± SD: the central tendency and spread — always report these together
  • Skewness / Kurtosis: both should be close to 0 for a normal distribution. Divide each value by its standard error to get a z-score; |z| > 3.29 flags a significant deviation (in large samples, use visual inspection instead)
  • Boxplot whiskers: extend to 1.5 × IQR; individual points beyond the whiskers are potential outliers
TipReporting in APA format

For continuous variables: M = [mean], SD = [SD], N = [n]. For frequency data: report counts and percentages.
