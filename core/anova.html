ANOVA

Modified

18 June 2026

Basics

Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) extends the logic of the t-test to situations with three or more groups, multiple factors, covariates, or repeated measurements. It answers the question: are there significant differences in means across conditions?

This page covers four ANOVA designs. Jump to the relevant section:

  • One-way ANOVA — one factor, three or more independent groups
  • ANCOVA — one factor with one or more continuous covariates
  • Factorial ANOVA — two or more factors (between-subjects)
  • Repeated-measures ANOVA — within-subjects or mixed designs

One-way ANOVA

TipResearch vignette — do the four Hogwarts houses differ in Machiavellianism?

Jakob et al. (2019) measured Machiavellianism in students who identified as Gryffindor, Hufflepuff, Ravenclaw, or Slytherin: four independent groups, one continuous personality scale. A one-way ANOVA asks whether any house differs from the others; a planned contrast (Slytherin vs the rest) tests the obvious follow-up. From Labcoat Leni (DSUJ ch. 11).

→ Worked in Exercise 1 below.

What is it?

One-way ANOVA tests whether the means of three or more independent groups differ on a continuous dependent variable. The “one-way” refers to a single grouping factor (independent variable).

The F-statistic is the ratio of between-groups variance to within-groups variance. A large F indicates that the group means are more spread out than expected by chance.

TipWhere the F-statistic comes from

F is the ratio of the mean squares of the model (how much variance the group means capture) to the mean squares of the error (leftover within-group variance). Like a t-statistic, F is a standardised test statistic with a known sampling distribution — that’s how a p-value gets attached to it.

Assumptions

  • Independence: each participant contributes data to only one group
  • Normality: the DV should be approximately normally distributed within each group (check with Q-Q plot of residuals)
  • Homogeneity of variance (homoscedasticity): the variance should be similar across groups — check with Levene’s test or observed group variances. If violated, use Welch’s ANOVA (available in JASP)
TipWhy assumptions actually matter (small demonstration)

When the DV is heavy-tailed (e.g. reaction times with extreme values), the empirical sampling distribution of F under H₀ no longer matches the theoretical F-distribution. The result: your α and power aren’t what you think they are. ANOVA is relatively robust to mild non-normality, but vulnerable to extreme observations that inflate within-group variance.

Effect size

Report η² (eta squared) or the less biased ω² (omega squared). For factorial designs, make sure to use the partial versions of these effect sizes (partial η² & partial ω²).

η² Conventional label
.01 Small
.06 Medium
.14 Large
TipEffect size vs. test statistic — what’s the difference?

A t (or F) statistic grows with both the magnitude of the effect and the sample size — increase n with a fixed mean difference and t climbs accordingly. Effect sizes like Cohen’s d, r, or η² are standardised and largely insulated from sample size. That’s why a significant result with a tiny effect size can still be practically meaningless, and why both belong in your report.

→ Worked example with numbers

Post-hoc tests

A significant F-test only tells you that at least one pair of groups differs — not which pairs. Request post-hoc tests (e.g., Tukey HSD) or planned contrasts to identify specific differences. JASP provides both.

How to do it in JASP

Go to ANOVA → ANOVA in the analysis menu.

ANCOVA (Analysis of Covariance)

TipResearch vignette — does interpretation training leave children with a negative bias?

Muris et al. (2008) randomly assigned children to one of three training conditions (negative / positive / no information about an ambiguous scenario), then measured an interpretation-bias score later. Age, gender, and natural anxiety vary between kids and would muddy a plain one-way ANOVA, so an ANCOVA pulls those covariates out and lets the training comparison stand on equal footing. From Labcoat Leni (DSUJ ch. 12).

→ Worked in Exercise 2 below.

What is it?

ANCOVA extends ANOVA by adding one or more continuous covariates — variables you want to statistically control for. By removing the variance explained by the covariate, ANCOVA gives a more sensitive test of the group effect.

Example: comparing therapy outcomes across three groups while accounting for pre-therapy symptom severity.

Key additional assumption

  • Homogeneity of regression slopes: the relationship between the covariate and the DV should be the same in all groups. JASP can test this by adding a Group × Covariate interaction — it should not be significant. If the interaction effect is significant, you need to take this into account when interpreting the main effect of group. For instance, this therapy is effective for people in group A, if they have low pre-therapy symptom severity. (Unfamiliar with what an “interaction” is? See the callout in the Factorial ANOVA section below.)
TipHow ANCOVA partitions variance (with worked example)

Once you add a covariate, the regression coefficients for the group dummies no longer recover the raw group means — they recover means adjusted for the covariate. The F-tests for group and covariate then come from comparing the full model against reduced models that drop one predictor at a time.

→ Worked example: extraversion ~ nationality + openness

How to do it in JASP

Add covariates in the Covariates box when setting up an ANOVA.

Factorial ANOVA

TipResearch vignette — does mood × “stop rule” affect compulsive checking?

Davey et al. (2003) crossed two factors — induced mood (negative vs positive) × stop-rule for an ambiguous task (“as many as can” / “feel like continuing” / no instruction) — and measured how much each participant checked. The substantive question is the interaction: does the effect of mood depend on which stop-rule you got? The two main effects on their own can be misleading when the interaction is doing the work. From Labcoat Leni (DSUJ ch. 13).

→ Worked in Exercise 3 below.

What is it?

Factorial ANOVA includes two or more between-subjects factors. In addition to the main effect of each factor, it tests the interaction — whether the effect of one factor depends on the level of another.

Example: testing whether a new intervention is effective (factor 1: intervention vs. control) and whether this depends on participant gender (factor 2: male/female/other).

Interpreting interactions

An interaction means the effect of factor A is different at different levels of factor B. Always examine interaction plots — JASP provides these under Descriptive plots. A significant interaction means you should interpret simple main effects rather than main effects alone.

TipWhat “interaction” really means

A concrete example: does the effect of alcohol on driving ability depend on the dose of speed (methamphetamine) the driver has also taken? Alcohol impairs driving on its own, and so does speed — the interaction term asks whether the two together do more (or less) damage than you’d guess from adding their separate effects. If somebody who’s taken a high dose of speed shows a bigger alcohol-related impairment than someone who hasn’t, that’s the interaction in action.

Repeated-measures ANOVA

TipResearch vignette — are splattered cadavers distracting during serial recall?

Each participant did a serial-recall task three times — once with no music, once with classical, once with death metal with explicit lyrics — so the music factor is within-subjects. A repeated-measures ANOVA uses the within-person correlation to ask whether genre actually shifts memory performance, with Mauchly’s test and a Greenhouse–Geisser correction handling the sphericity assumption that comes in once you have three or more levels. From Labcoat Leni (DSUJ ch. 14).

→ Worked in Exercise 4 below.

What is it?

Repeated-measures ANOVA is used when the same participants are measured across multiple conditions or time points (within-subjects design). Because measurements within a person are correlated, this design is more powerful than a between-subjects design of the same size.

Mixed designs contain both a within-subjects factor (e.g., time: pre, post, follow-up) and a between-subjects factor (e.g., treatment group).

Key additional assumption: sphericity

For within-subjects factors with three or more levels, ANOVA assumes sphericity — that the variances of the differences between all pairs of levels are equal. JASP performs Mauchly’s test automatically:

  • If sphericity is violated (p < .05): use the Greenhouse-Geisser or Huynh-Feldt correction (JASP offers both)
  • If there are only two within-subjects levels, sphericity cannot be violated (it is automatic)
TipSphericity, Mauchly, and “just always apply the correction”

Sphericity = the variances of the differences between every pair of within-subject levels are equal. Mauchly’s test flags violations, but (like Levene’s and Shapiro-Wilk) it’s sensitive to sample size. The pragmatic move: under Assumption Checks → Sphericity corrections, tick Greenhouse-Geisser (or Huynh-Feldt) by default — they adjust the degrees of freedom in the ANOVA table to give you a result that’s robust to sphericity violations whether or not Mauchly is significant.

Interpreting the ANOVA output

The ANOVA table shows:

Column What it means
F Test statistic — variance between groups / variance within groups
df Degrees of freedom — (number of groups − 1) for the effect, (N − number of groups) for the error
p Significance — reject H₀ if p < α
η² or ω² Effect size
TipAPA reporting

F(df_effect, df_error) = [value], p = [value], η² = [value].

Example: F(2, 57) = 4.32, p = .018, η² = .13.

For repeated measures with a correction: F(1.76, 46.5) = 5.21, p = .011, η² = .17 (Greenhouse-Geisser corrected).

Non-parametric alternatives

If ANOVA assumptions are seriously violated:

ANOVA design Non-parametric alternative
One-way (between) Kruskal–Wallis test
One-way (within) Friedman test

Both are available under ANOVA → Nonparametric in JASP. See the Non-Parametric Tests page for the full walkthrough.
