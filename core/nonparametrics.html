Non-Parametric Tests

Modified

18 June 2026

Basics

Non-parametric tests are rank-based alternatives to t-tests and ANOVA. They don’t assume the data follow a particular distribution — making them the go-to choice when the normality assumption is seriously violated (often in small samples, or with strong outliers or floor/ceiling effects).

This page covers the four most commonly used non-parametric tests for group comparison, each one a counterpart to a parametric procedure you already know:

Parametric test Non-parametric equivalent Design
Independent-samples t-test Wilcoxon rank-sum test (Mann–Whitney U) Two independent groups
Paired-samples t-test Wilcoxon signed-rank test Two related measurements
One-way ANOVA (between) Kruskal–Wallis test Three or more independent groups
Repeated-measures ANOVA Friedman test Three or more related measurements

Research vignettes — what each test can answer

TipWilcoxon rank-sum / Mann–Whitney — do men and women spend different amounts of time shopping?

A two-group comparison where the outcome (minutes per shopping trip) is heavily right-skewed: most trips are short, a few are very long. Means get pulled around by the long-tail trips; ranks don’t. The Mann–Whitney asks whether trip durations rank systematically higher in one group than the other. From DSUJ ch. 15 (Smart Alex 15.7).

→ Worked in Exercise 3 below.

TipWilcoxon signed-rank — do male quails fertilise more eggs after a signaled vs control mating context?

Each male quail experienced both a signaled and a control mating context, in counterbalanced order, with the number of fertilised eggs counted each time. Two related measurements per bird, small n, count data — the conditions in which the paired t-test starts creaking and the signed-rank test on the within-bird differences is the safer call. From Labcoat Leni (DSUJ ch. 15).

→ Worked in Exercise 1 below.

TipKruskal–Wallis — do three “quail fetish” subtypes differ in fertilisation rate?

Male quails were classified into three behavioural subtypes around a terrycloth object and compared on subsequent fertilisation rate: three independent groups, small n, count outcome. The Kruskal–Wallis is the rank-based one-way ANOVA; when it comes back significant, Dunn’s pairwise post-hoc tests with a Bonferroni correction give you the per-pair detail. From Labcoat Leni (DSUJ ch. 15).

→ Worked in Exercise 2 below.

TipFriedman — does a diet program shift body weight across three measurement waves?

The same participants weighed at baseline, after one month, and after two months of a structured diet: three repeated measurements per person, weight changes that are often skewed, and an n too small to lean on the central limit theorem. The Friedman test ranks each person’s three measurements within the person, then asks whether those rank distributions differ across time points — the rank-based counterpart to a within-subjects one-way ANOVA. From DSUJ ch. 15 (within-subjects diet example).

What do they actually do?

All four tests follow the same idea: convert your raw scores to ranks, then test whether the ranks differ between groups (or conditions). Because ranks are bounded and don’t depend on the original metric, the resulting test statistic isn’t pulled around by outliers or skew the way a t or F statistic can be.

TipWhy “rank” the data?

Imagine one participant in your dataset reports a reaction time of 12,000 ms while everyone else is between 200–800 ms. That single value drags the mean of their group up and inflates the variance of the difference — your t drops, your p climbs, and you may miss a real effect. Replace each raw score by its rank (1 = fastest, N = slowest), and that outlier becomes “the slowest one” — its influence is bounded. You trade away some information (the absolute spacing of the values) for robustness against extreme scores.

When to use a non-parametric test

  • The normality assumption of the parametric test is seriously violated, especially in small samples (n < ~30) where you can’t rely on the central limit theorem
  • You have ordinal data (e.g. Likert items treated strictly as ranks), where means aren’t really meaningful
  • You have strong outliers that you don’t want to remove and that distort the parametric test
WarningNon-parametric ≠ assumption-free

The Mann–Whitney test does not test for equal medians unless the two distributions have the same shape. If group A is symmetric and group B is heavily skewed, a significant Mann–Whitney result could reflect that difference in shape — not a median shift. Always inspect the distributions before interpreting.

How to do them in JASP

In JASP, every non-parametric test lives alongside its parametric counterpart:

  • Wilcoxon rank-sum / signed-rank → T-Tests menu → tick the Wilcoxon option
  • Kruskal–Wallis → ANOVA menu → tick the Kruskal-Wallis test option (under Nonparametrics)
  • Friedman → Repeated Measures ANOVA menu → tick the Friedman option

JASP reports the rank-based statistic, a p-value, and a rank-biserial correlation as the effect size for the Wilcoxon tests.

For the Wilcoxon tests there is nothing new to show: it is the Wilcoxon (Mann–Whitney) checkbox in the same Independent Samples T-Test panel you already use for the parametric test. The Kruskal–Wallis test is the one that looks a little different, since it sits in the Nonparametrics section of the ordinary ANOVA dialog:

Interpreting the output

Test Statistic Effect size
Wilcoxon rank-sum W (or U) rank-biserial correlation
Wilcoxon signed-rank V rank-biserial correlation (matched-rank)
Kruskal–Wallis H (≈ χ²) ε² or η²H
Friedman χ²F Kendall’s W

Report the test statistic, p-value, and an effect size. Where relevant, also report group medians (and IQRs) rather than means.

TipAPA reporting example

A Wilcoxon rank-sum test showed that reaction times were significantly higher in the alcohol condition (Mdn = 540, IQR = 180) than in the control condition (Mdn = 420, IQR = 140), W = 312, p = .008, rank-biserial r = .42.

Post-hoc tests

A significant Kruskal–Wallis or Friedman test tells you that at least two groups (conditions) differ — but not which. JASP offers Dunn’s post-hoc tests with Bonferroni or Holm correction directly in the same dialog.
