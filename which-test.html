Which test should I use?

Modified

18 June 2026

Reference

Picking the right analysis is mostly a matter of answering four questions about your design and your variables. The flowchart below walks through the most common cases; if your design fits neatly into one of them, the page link at the bottom of each branch is where to go next.

TipThe four questions
  1. What is your research question? Hypothesis testing, parameter estimation, classifying people, or predicting an outcome?
  2. What is the measurement level of your outcome variable? Categorical (nominal) or continuous (scale)?
  3. How many predictor variables are involved and what is their measurement level? Categorical (nominal), continuous (scale), or mixed?
  4. Are the groups independent or related? Between-subjects (different people) or within-subjects (same people, repeated)? In repeated designs, the outcome variable is observed multiple times for each participant, so the observations are not independent.

Some practical rules of thumb

NoteMatch the design, not just the data

The most common student mistake is to pick a test based on the outcome variable alone and ignore the design. Two questionnaire scores from the same person are not two independent observations — even though the spreadsheet has two columns. Always identify whether your rows are independent before picking a test.

NoteWhen in doubt, default to the linear model

Most of the inferential tests above are the same model underneath — the linear model with different choices of outcome distribution and link function. A t-test is a regression with one binary predictor; an ANOVA is regression with dummy-coded categorical predictors; logistic regression is the linear model with a logit link. If a textbook test doesn’t quite fit your situation, the linear-model framing usually has an answer.

→ For a worked walkthrough of how dummy coding turns categorical predictors into regression coefficients, see Predicting with dummies — one categorical predictor.
